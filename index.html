Executive Summary

◈
2025 Operational Overview: Significant H2 Recovery After a Severe H1

1,183 helpdesk tickets were recorded across 38 categories in 2025, generating approximately 255,866 hours of cumulative downtime. The first half of the year was dramatically worse — accounting for 63% of all downtime — but a sustained H2 improvement reduced total hours by 41.8%. Three categories (Permissions, Onboarding, Prod Machine) drove 29% of all downtime hours. January and June represent the two highest-risk months and warrant structural intervention.

Critical Driver: Permissions & Access (32,230 hrs)

The single largest downtime category. 168 tickets averaging 201 hours each. Predominantly Access/Connection Issues (15,815 hrs) and General permissions failures (8,924 hrs). Systemic RBAC and provisioning deficiencies.

Highest-Risk Period: January 2025 (z = +1.81)

37,995 hours — the worst month on record. Driven by cascading file system failures, connection issues, and a missing-files incident. Post-holiday staffing trough compounded by concurrent system upgrades.

Resolution Outliers Skewing All Metrics

Mean TTR is 228 hours (9.5 days), but the median is only 74 hours. The top 1% of tickets each exceed 3,016 hours. Ten incidents alone contributed over 44,700 hours — 17.5% of annual downtime.

Strong H2 Improvement — But Fragile

H2 downtime fell 41.8% vs H1 (94,169 vs 161,697 hours). Monthly ticket counts stabilised at ~90–100. The June spike (z = +1.25) mid-trend warns that underlying causes are not fully resolved.

Key Insights

F01
Permissions is the single largest downtime driver, not the most acute incident category

168 tickets generated 32,230 hours — the highest of any category. Root cause is structural: absence of automated role provisioning forces manual workflows that stall for days. This is a process failure, not a technology failure.

→ Implement RBAC automation with a sub-4-hour SLA for access requests.
F02
Onboarding has the highest average resolution time (884 hrs = 37 days) of any category

22 tickets account for 19,438 hrs — nearly as much downtime as Prod Machine (77 tickets). New employees spend over a month without full system access. This is a silent productivity drain invisible to operational metrics but severe in human cost.

→ Design a parallel-track onboarding workflow targeting 5-day completion (120 hrs).
F03
The mean TTR (228 hrs) is 3× the median (74 hrs) — a classic long-tail distribution

The gap is driven by a small number of chronic, unresolved tickets, primarily in Security, Onboarding, Machine, and File categories. Managing P99 outliers will have a greater impact on mean TTR than broad process improvements.

→ Introduce an escalation trigger at 168 hrs (7 days) for all open tickets.
F04
Shift 3 generates 45.8% of all downtime despite handling 36.1% of tickets

The resolution time gap (282 hrs vs 184 hrs for Shift 2) is 53%. The Shift 3 + Permissions combination alone contributes 15,793 hrs — 6.2% of all annual downtime. A staffing or skills deficit on Shift 3 is the most likely cause.

→ Audit Shift 3 staffing levels, tooling access, and category-specific training.
F05
H1 2025 accounted for 63.2% of annual downtime — a front-loaded risk year

January–June generated 161,697 hrs vs 94,169 hrs in H2. The H2 improvement (−41.8%) is real but the root causes of H1 severity — cascading file system and permission failures — have not been formally closed out.

→ Document all H1 major incidents with root cause closure and preventive actions by Q2 2026.
F06
Aspera Console/HSTS failures recurred three times without root cause resolution

Three separate key tickets reference the same London Aspera Console/HSTS issue on different dates. Each was logged as a new incident rather than a known issue in progress. Certificate expiry management is absent.

→ Implement 30-day advance cert expiry alerting; create a known-issue registry.
F07
Three uncontrolled external dependencies caused multi-facility outages

AWS US-East-1 (15.2 hrs), SohoNet West LA (21.5 hrs), and Burbank power (20 hrs) are uncontrolled dependencies with no documented failover or redundancy. All three caused facility-wide outages affecting multiple downstream systems simultaneously.

→ Develop continuity plans for each: AWS multi-region, secondary ISP contracts, UPS/generator coverage.
F08
Security has the highest average TTR (897 hrs) despite only 9 tickets

The Bitwarden Org License incident (5,610 hrs) drove this figure. Low frequency masks extreme impact. License management for critical security tools is not monitored proactively — a renewal failure cascades immediately to all credential-dependent systems.

→ Add all security-critical license renewals to an automated monitoring calendar.
F09
Admin / Data Management is the most common subcategory (203 tickets) — a systemic ops gap

203 tickets categorised as administrative data management tasks points to insufficient self-service tooling. These tickets span multiple categories (Permissions, Drives/Volumes, Prod Machine, Software) — they are process tickets, not fault tickets. Every one represents a manual intervention that could be automated.

→ Conduct a self-service automation audit across the top 5 Admin/Data Mgmt ticket categories.
F10
June 2025 anomaly (z = +1.25) immediately after a 3-month improvement trend is a structural warning

May was the best month to that point (11,890 hrs). June reverted sharply to 32,847 hrs. This is not a random fluctuation — it corresponds temporally with long-tail incidents from Q1 closing and Onboarding backlogs cresting. Improvement trends can reverse without structural fixes.

→ Establish a monthly operations review with a downtime threshold alert (>20,000 hrs triggers escalation).
F11
Jesus Serrano submitted 100 tickets in 2025 — 8.5% of total volume from a single requester

The top 10 requesters submitted 513 tickets (43.4% of volume). High requester concentration may indicate a specific team, role, or workflow with disproportionate technical friction, or it may reflect a power user serving as a proxy for a larger team.

→ Analyse the top requester patterns to identify whether training, tooling, or process changes can reduce ticket generation.
F12
5.2% missing duration data undermines SLA reporting accuracy

62 tickets have no resolution time recorded. Several fall in high-priority categories (AWS: 8, Permissions: 8). SLA compliance calculations using this dataset will overstate performance by masking potentially slow resolutions in the missing records.

→ Enforce mandatory duration capture at ticket close via system-level validation; backfill using linked systems.
